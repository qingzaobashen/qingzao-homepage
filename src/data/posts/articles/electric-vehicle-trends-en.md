# Electric Vehicles: You Don't Need to Follow Every Brand, Just Understand These 5 Things

90% of what you see about EVs is noise. Reviewers talk specs, manufacturers pitch technology, and media write stories. But as a consumer, you only need to understand 5 things.

---

## 1. Pure EV or Hybrid? Three Conditions

### Go Pure EV if You Meet 2 of 3

1. **You have your own charger** (parking spot where you can install one)
2. **Daily commute under 200km** (covers 99% of daily scenarios)
3. **Rarely drive over 400km one way**

### Choose Hybrid When

- No fixed charging spot
- Frequent long trips (500km+ one way)
- License plate restrictions in your city (hybrids sometimes have policy benefits)

### Common Mistake

Thinking "I occasionally take long trips, so I need a hybrid." But if you take long trips fewer than 5 times a year, pure EV + train/car rental is actually cheaper.

---

## 2. Focus on the Battery, Not the "Range Number"

Range is the spec manufacturers love to exaggerate. Real-world range is typically 70-80% of the advertised number (even lower in winter).

### Truly Important Battery Specs

**1. Battery Chemistry**

- **LFP (Lithium Iron Phosphate)**: cheaper, safer, longer lifespan, but lower energy density, worse winter performance. Good for southern users or those on a budget.
- **NCM (Nickel Cobalt Manganese)**: higher energy density, better range, better cold weather performance, but more expensive, slightly shorter cycle life. Better for northern users or range-sensitive buyers.

**2. Battery Capacity (kWh)**

This is the most useful spec:

- 40-50kWh: city commuting (Wuling Mini EV, BYD Dolphin)
- 60-80kWh: mainstream family cars (Model 3, BYD Han, XPeng P7)
- 100kWh+: long range / luxury (Model S, NIO ET7)

Don't just look at "600km range." Check the battery capacity and real-world reputation.

**3. Charging Speed**

- AC slow charging (home): 7kW, full overnight
- DC fast charging (public): look at peak power and the charging curve

Many cars claim "200kW fast charging," but actual average power from 10%-80% might be 60-80kW. What matters is **10%-80% charge time**: under 30 minutes is acceptable, under 20 minutes is excellent.

## 3. Autonomous Driving: Don't Be Fooled by "L2+" Buzzwords

### Reality

- **L2** (adaptive cruise + lane keeping): available on all mainstream EVs, works on highways
- **L2+** (auto lane change + auto park + navigation-assisted driving): Huawei, XPeng, and Tesla do this well; others vary widely
- **L3 and above**: no truly commercial L3 exists yet (regulatory and technical hurdles remain)

### Buying Advice

- Don't spend thousands extra for "self-driving" — no car truly self-drives yet
- But good driver assistance genuinely reduces fatigue
- How to tell? Test drive on a highway for 20 minutes

---

## 4. Charging: The #1 Factor in EV Satisfaction

### If You Drive Mostly in the City

The key isn't charging station density — it's **whether you can install a charger where you live**.

With home charging: EV experience 10/10
Without home charging: EV experience 5/10

It's that simple.

### If You Rely on Public Charging

- Download "Teld," "Star Charge," or "State Grid" apps to check nearby stations
- Prioritize 120kW+ chargers (60kW takes an hour for a half charge)
- Avoid peak hours (many stations have queues at 6-8pm)

### Road Trip Planning

- Most highway service areas now have chargers (85%+ coverage)
- Plan your route with charging stops marked
- Keep 30% range buffer (calculate based on 70% of advertised range)

## 5. Cost: EVs Are Cheaper, But Not on Every Line Item

### Cost Comparison (20,000km/year, 5 years)

| Item | Gas Car | EV |
|------|---------|-----|
| Fuel/Energy | 12,000元/year | 2,400元/year |
| Maintenance | 3,000元/year | 600元/year |
| Insurance | 4,000元/year | 4,500元/year |
| 5-year total | 95,000元 | 37,500元 |

**Save about 57,000元 over 5 years.**

But note:

- EV insurance is indeed 10-20% more expensive
- EV resale value is lower (≈50% after 3 years vs ≈65% for gas cars)
- Battery degradation (most mainstream brands offer 8-year/160,000km warranty, so generally not a concern)

---

## 6. When You Should NOT Buy an EV

- No fixed parking spot (can't install a charger)
- Building doesn't have enough electrical capacity (HOA won't let you install)
- Your city stays below -10°C for 3+ months in winter
- You frequently drive to remote areas (Tibet, Xinjiang, most of Qinghai)

If any of these applies, stick with gas or hybrid.

---

## Conclusion

EVs aren't perfect, but if your usage fits the criteria, the experience improvement and cost savings are real.

Before buying, run your use case against each of the points above — it'll be more useful than reading 100 reviews.
