# Complete Decoration Guide for Beginners: From Rough House to Move-in

Decoration can be a complex process for beginners. This guide provides a complete walkthrough from rough house to move-in, helping you systematically plan every phase.

## 1 Introduction
1. Overall phases: 1) Preparation; 2) Hard decoration; 3) Soft furnishing;
2. Decoration characteristics: non-standard, low-frequency, complex;
3. Three core needs: safety, functionality, aesthetics;
## 2 Preparation Phase
### 2.1 Basic Concepts
1. Water/electric/masonry/wood/paint: water is the blood, electricity the nerves, masonry the skeleton, paint the skin;
2. The building process is construction, the materials used are decoration materials;
3. Invisible materials are auxiliary materials, e.g. pipes, wires, cement, mortar;
4. Visible materials are main materials, e.g. tiles, flooring, sanitary ware;
5. Design determines construction and materials; construction and materials affect the result;
6. Full-service: construction + materials (auxiliary + main); Half-service: construction + auxiliary; Labor-only: construction only;
7. If you turn the house upside down, anything that falls off is soft furnishing, anything that stays is hard decoration;
### 2.2 House Inspection
1. Tools: tape measure + hollow sound hammer;
2. Developer must provide: Quality Guarantee, User Manual, Construction Quality Certificate; Comprehensive Acceptance Certificate; Completion Filing Form;
3. Inspect before signing and paying; rainy days are best for detecting leaks;
4. Check doors and windows: appearance, operation, damage, missing parts, stiffness;
5. Floors and walls: tap with hammer, check for cracks;
6. For walls already plastered, wet them to see if the putty washes off — if not, it's water-resistant (can be left as-is);
7. Plumbing: pour water down drains to check flow; check pipe integrity;
8. Check flue, AC duct holes, and outdoor unit placement;
9. Measure every dimension you can, imagine your future life there;
### 2.3 Design Planning
1. Two parts: design and budget;
2. Design it yourself first — only you know your life;
3. A house is for living in, and only secondly for looking at;
4. Who lives with you, lifestyle habits, hobbies, cooking style, laundry frequency — write down every detail;
5. Reactive aesthetics (sensory, no right/wrong) vs reflective aesthetics (has good/bad);
6. Tools like Kujiale; if your designer uses the same tools as you (Kujiale, Sanweijia), they're not much more advanced; professional designers use CAD, 3ds Max, etc.;
### 2.4 Budget Mastery
1. Design fees are justified — 20+ years of professional learning and experience can't be understood in a few days;
2. Base decoration: labor + auxiliary materials;
3. HVAC: underfloor heating, central AC — these need separate orders during hard decoration;
4. Doors and windows: balcony sealing, window replacement, security doors, entry doors — not including interior doors;
5. Main materials: 1) Doors: kitchen, bedroom, bathroom, balcony; 2) Flooring: tiles, planks, skirting, grout; 3) Walls: tiles, wallpaper, latex paint; 4) Sanitary ware, lighting, switches;
6. Custom cabinetry: kitchen cabinets, shoe cabinets, wardrobes, wine cabinets, TV cabinets, sideboards, balcony cabinets, custom desks, tatami;
7. Ready-made furniture: sofas, coffee tables, dining tables, chairs, mattresses;
8. Appliances: TV, fridge, washer, range hood, stove, AC, water dispenser, microwave, water heater;
9. Decor: artwork, rugs, vases, etc.;
10. Saving principles: cost-performance, experience, long-term cost, safety cost;
11. It's a one-off transaction — the contractor won't handle after-sales;
12. For high-tech products choose big brands (e.g. appliances); for mature-tech products, smaller factories are better value;
13. Highly personalized services (underfloor heating, windows, AC) buy offline (negotiable + customizable); standardized items (main materials, furniture) can be bought online;
14. Smart home: smart devices (locks, vacuums, toilets) are mature; smart ecosystems for on/off control are sufficient;
## 3 Hard Decoration Phase
### 3.1 Design
1. Go to designers and contractors with your plans, budget, and ideas;
2. Complete design includes: floor plan, construction drawings, renderings; construction drawings cover floors, ceilings, plumbing, electrical, lighting;
3. Get a valid quote based on the design drawings;
4. Before finalizing the design, lock in HVAC, windows, and cabinetry vendors, visit the site with the designer to confirm equipment placement and cabinet dimensions;
### 3.2 Choosing a Contractor
1. Take time to compare calmly — visit sites, check portfolios, compare quotes and professionalism;
2. Half-service: let workers handle the work; your job is to manage the process, understand what they're doing and why, avoid being misled, and know the key points;
3. About project supervisors: mostly a placebo — learn the acceptance standards yourself;
### 3.3 HVAC
1. Besides construction, HVAC, windows, and cabinetry need to be arranged early;
2. HVAC includes AC, underfloor heating, water purification — usually one vendor handles it all;
3. AC types: split, ducted, central;
4. Floor heating: water vs electric; water is the standard choice, electric is for occasional use;
5. Ventilation: ducted for large villas, wall-mounted for regular homes;
6. Water purification: whole-house system filters sediment, softener removes scale, drinking system filters bacteria; a backwash pre-filter is recommended;
### 3.4 Doors and Windows
1. Requirements: insulation, soundproofing, durability — meaning slow heat transfer, good sealing, high strength;
2. Types: sliding (poor sealing) vs casement; materials: PVC/steel (good insulation, low strength), aluminum (strong, poor insulation — rarely used), thermal-break aluminum (excellent but expensive);
### 3.5 Hard Decoration Process
1. Water → electric → masonry → wood → paint (order of wood vs masonry may vary);
2. Masonry: backfill, leveling, sloping, waterproofing, tiling;
3. Wood: ceilings, door/window trimming, wall paneling;
4. Paint: putty, wall paint;
#### 3.5.1 Preparation
1. Register with property management, pay deposit;
2. Wall/floor sealant: yellow walls, green floors — locks surface dust for better adhesion; apply before work starts, ideally before putty/tiling; ~100 RMB per bucket; do after demolition and wall scraping;
3. Marking: label all switches, outlets, lighting, ceiling tracks, curtain boxes, furniture/appliance positions; mark demolition/new wall lines, ceiling finish lines, door opening lines, leveling lines, tiling lines;
#### 3.5.2 Demolition
1. Demolition: remove, build, patch;
2. Protect areas first with plasterboard and film;
3. Scrape off non-water-resistant wall putty;
4. Remove insulation walls if: poor quality, space-saving, or kitchen/bathroom tiling areas;
5. Remove windows after woodwork phase;
6. Use solid bricks for walls, or aerated bricks + solid bricks; rebar insertion, staggered joints, and structural columns are standard for quality work;
7. Have the designer re-measure after demolition;
#### 3.5.3 Plumbing & Electrical
1. Coordinate AC, underfloor heating, water purification, and cabinetry vendors for a joint site meeting;
2. Water: supply and drainage; supply uses 25mm PPR pipes (hot left, cold right); pressure-test after installation;
3. Drainage must not clog or smell; PVC pipes: 110mm for sewage, 75mm for drains, 50mm for kitchen waste; wall-floor vs straight-floor drainage; same-floor drainage should have secondary drains;
4. Electrical: high voltage and low voltage;
5. Low voltage: Cat6 or Cat6a Ethernet; pre-run fiber optic; leave network cables in kitchen and bathroom;
6. High voltage: use BV single-strand wire (BVR stranded not needed); 1.5/2.5/4 sq mm wires; current capacity: ≤2.5mm × 9, ≥4mm × 8/7/6; multiply by voltage for max wattage;
7. Circuit sizing: lighting 1.5mm, general outlets 2.5mm, kitchen/bathroom 4mm, standard AC 4mm, central AC 6mm; ground wire 2.5mm; smart switches with neutral need a neutral wire;
8. PVC conduits for wiring; test all circuits after completion;
9. Get a quote based on actual design — electrical work is fast and complexity doesn't greatly affect material/labor costs;
10. Run wires on ceiling if worried about issues, on floor for better quality;
11. Water pipe bends should be right angles for straight runs; wall wires also need straight runs for easy identification;
12. Take plenty of photos after completion for future maintenance;
13. Order: site meeting → channel cutting → drilling → wiring → point placement → pre-filter installation → circuit test → water pressure test → pre-embedding → floor heating → ~7 working days;
#### 3.5.4 Masonry
1. Masonry is the most expensive trade;
2. Includes: wall building, pipe wrapping, backfill, leveling, sloping, waterproofing, tiling;
3. Backfill methods: construction waste, ceramsite, foam cement, carbon pellets, or elevated void; foam cement is recommended;
4. No need to level before tiling — level during tiling; but underfloor heating needs special concrete leveling;
5. Slope the bathroom floor before waterproofing and tiling;
6. Waterproofing: focus on drain areas — water can't drain elsewhere even if waterproofed;
7. After waterproofing, fill the bathroom with water for 48 hours, check the level hasn't dropped;
8. Flooring: engineered wood for heated floors; laminate is low quality, solid wood needs waxing;
9. Choose small brands' high-end tiles;
10. Larger tiles → higher density → lower water absorption → better quality → higher price;
11. Wall tiles: large-format tiles need tile adhesive (expensive); ceramic tiles are less attractive — vendors push large-format tiles;
12. Dry-lay tiles for trial, then use leveling tools;
13. Inside corners: 45° cut or mitre joint with grout;
14. Apply grout twice;
15. Check for R-angle defects — depression at tile edges causing a ridge when joined;
#### 3.5.5 Woodwork
1. Typically just ceilings and curtain boxes;
2. Standard and waterproof plasterboard;
3. Use galvanized steel studs in kitchens;
4. Masonry + woodwork: ~15 working days;
#### 3.5.6 Paint
1. Choose major brands for latex paint; don't chase imported brands;
2. Apply 2-3 coats of putty for leveling, sand, one primer coat, two top coats;
3. Mesh tape over新旧 wall joints and channeled areas before puttying to prevent cracking;
4. Use water-based ready-mix putty;

#### 3.6.3 Interior Doors
1. Bedroom doors are typically wooden;
2. Solid wood vs engineered solid wood;
3. Engineered solid wood: solid wood veneer over a core of solid timber blocks (preferred);
4. Door frame core should be the best quality, door leaf next, door casing last;
5. Finish: lacquer > paint > wood veneer > PVC > melamine paper;
6. Best: all solid wood frame, leaf, and casing; composite doors should have solid wood-filled core with wood veneer;
7. Single-wrap vs double-wrap: single wraps one side (the other side gets tiled), double wraps both sides (like bedroom doors);
#### 3.6.4 Skirting
1. Best matched to door frame color;
2. For a skirting-free look (expensive), plan early with the designer;
3. Materials: PVC, bamboo-wood fiber, metal, tile, solid wood — first three use clips; tile is durable but hard to match door frames; solid wood is common, use invisible nails for secure installation;
#### 3.6.5 Tiles
#### 3.6.6 Flooring
1. Laminate: MDF base + melamine paper + aluminum oxide wear layer — essentially wear-resistant paint-free MDF. Pros: cheap, durable, stable. Cons: not moisture-proof, high glue content, not for heated floors.
2. Quality check: thickness (8-12mm recommended), wear rating (Chinese Grade I / European AC3+).
3. Engineered wood: multi-layer (plywood base + wood veneer) or 3-layer (top/bottom wood planks, middle strips). Pros: real wood grain, low glue content, some moisture resistance. Cons: surface not wear-resistant, needs maintenance, pricier.
4. Quality: wood species, veneer thickness (thicker = better), wear rating.
5. WPC: wood + plastic, contains glue, not wear-resistant, expensive, unproven.
6. SPC: stone + plastic, tile-like properties, great value, dense, waterproof, wear-resistant, glue-free, formaldehyde-free, conducts heat well for heated floors, thin — saves height. But edges chip easily, feels hard underfoot, requires very flat subfloor.
## 4 Soft Furnishing Phase
Recommended book: "The Small Home, Bigger Life"
### 4.1 Custom Cabinetry
1. Decision method: 1) Real need; 2) How to achieve it; 3) Downsides of the approach.
2. Two options: custom cabinetry vendor vs on-site woodwork.
3. Components: design +板材 + fabrication (cutting, drilling, edge-banding) + hardware + installation.
4. Vendor = full-service hard decoration; on-site = labor-only.
#### 4.1.1 Design
1. Purpose: fit room and item dimensions, suit lifestyle, meet aesthetic requirements.
2. Self-planning: consider lifestyle, storage needs, built-in appliances, style (sketch with apps like Kujiale).
3. Designer selection: evaluate communication and portfolio. Self-planning may eliminate the need for a designer if detailed enough.
#### 4.1.2 Board Materials
1. Board = core (filling) + finish (surface).
2. Core = wood + glue; differentiated by wood form (powder,颗粒, strips, sheets, species) and glue type/quantity.
3. Finish: wood veneer, paper, PVC, lacquer, paint, wood wax oil.
4. Performance depends on core and finish materials and工艺.
5. No single best board — choose based on needs.
6. Selection dimensions:
   - **环保性**: less glue = more环保. Formaldehyde class: GB/T39600-2021 — E1 ≤0.124mg/m³, E0 ≤0.050mg/m³, ENF ≤0.025mg/m³.
   - **Stability**: affected by core structure.
   - **Screw-holding**: affected by wood strength, density, hardness, elasticity, grain, moisture content.
   - **Moisture resistance**: affected by density and finish.
   - **Aesthetics**: finish pattern, wood grain, color, surface carving.
   - **Price**: standard sheet 1.22×2.44m. Vendors quote by展开 area or projection area (projection ≈ 4×展开).
7. Board types:
   - **Particleboard**: wood particles + glue. Poor structure, weak screw-holding,不耐潮. Mediocre, cheap.
   - **OSB (Oriented Strand Board)**: large flakes + MDI glue. Better环保, strong screw-holding, used for wall backing. Slightly pricier.
   - **MDF**: wood powder + glue. High glue content, easy to carve,不耐潮. Used for door panels or back panels.
   - **Plywood**: thin wood veneers + glue. 9-11 layers typical, less glue, good物理性能,耐潮. Slightly expensive, prone to warping with too much glue — used for cabinet bodies, not doors.
   - **Blockboard**: softwood blocks glued into panels, thin veneer top/bottom. Easy to work, poor物理性能.
   - **Solid wood board**: multiple wood pieces + glue. No贴面, typically open-pore lacquer or wax oil. Edge-glued (直拼, better) or finger-jointed (指接).
8. Terminology notes:
   - Old standard GB/T28202-2011 abolished.
   - "实木" = real wood. Solid wood boards = multiple pieces joined.
   - "原木" =原始木材. Solid wood lumber = **single完整 piece of wood.**
   - Finger-jointed and edge-glued boards are called "实木板".
   - New standard GB/T28202-2020 removed "原木" — all are "实木家具".
   (Summary)
   In板材 marketing, "实木颗粒板" and similar terms are meaningless.
   - 实木板: finger-jointed or edge-glued.
   - 原木板: single完整 board (nearly unavailable).
   
   In furniture:
   - 实木类 furniture: uses实木 materials.
   - 实木家具: main parts made of实木 (少量人造板 OK).
   - 纯实木家具: all parts实木 (not in national standard; only 全实木 and 覆面实木 exist).
   - 全实木家具: solid wood with no surface treatment.
   - 覆面实木家具: solid wood with surface treatment.
   - 人造板家具: made of man-made boards (first 5 types above).
   
   Price varies by region. Particleboard and plywood are preferred choices.
9. Board selection plans:
   - Plan A (not recommended): all particleboard — poor moisture resistance.
   - Plan B (recommended): plywood for kitchen/bathroom bodies, particleboard elsewhere.
   - Plan C (recommended): plywood for all bodies, particleboard for all doors.
10. Brands:
    - Imported: Egger (Austria), Cleaf (Italy), Kronospan (Germany).
    - Domestic: Tubao,千年舟, Moganshan, Lushuihe, Daya, Dawangye, Wanhua.
    - Local brands with long history and some reputation.
11. Thickness: typically 18mm; back panels 9mm if saving space.
12. Finishes:
    -贴面: melamine paper, PVC film, PET吸塑, wood veneer. Most boards are贴面 — called "免漆板".
    - Paint: lacquer, paint, wax oil — lacquer is expensive (like car paint).
13. Painting:
    - Clear vs tinted paint: clear preserves wood grain, tinted covers it.
    - Open-pore vs closed-pore: open reveals grain, closed hides it.
    - Water-based vs oil-based: water-based (thinned with water) is more环保 and主流; oil-based uses thinner.
    - Paint forms a film; wax oil penetrates the wood — more环保 and natural-looking.
#### 4.1.3 Fabrication
1. Cutting, drilling, edge-banding.
2. Edge-banding (critical): seals exposed board edges.
   Methods:
   - EVA hot melt: high glue, not环保, visible glue line, prone to cracking.
   - **PUR hot melt**: opposite of EVA, replacing it — great value.
   - Laser: best, expensive.
   On-site手工 cutting精度 is lower than machine.
#### 4.1.4 Hardware
1. Hardware and countertops.
2. Hardware: hinges, hanging rods, handles, slides, pull-out baskets, sink faucets — affects daily use, confirm pricing.
   Brands: domestic: DTC (recommended), Hangg高, Dinggu, Nomi; imported: Blum (Austria), Grass, Hettich (Germany), Hafele, Salice (Italy).
3. Countertops: only quartz recommended. Stainless steel not recommended. Sintered stone is expensive (Guanzhu, Yinuo, Zhongsheng, Delifeng, Monalisa — 2000-3000 RMB/m²).
4. Sink: stainless steel under-mount.
5. Basin: ceramic一体 or Corian一体 or sintered stone.
#### 4.1.5 Installation
1. Check板材 and hardware match your selection, no color差, correct dimensions, and secure installation.
#### 4.1.6 Finding a Vendor
1. For convenience over cost: Sofiya, Oppein.
2. For saving money: have the vendor source through their channels, control板材 and hardware yourself.
3. Find custom cabinetry OEM factories via map search; check scale and reputation.
4. Factory visit checklist: 1) CNC cutting — smooth edges without chipping; 2) Edge-banding on white boards — smooth, round, no glue line; 3) CNC 6-sided drill; 4) Environmental certificates.
### 4.2 Furniture
#### 4.2.1 Ready-made Furniture
1. Often better工艺 and环保 than custom.
2. Reduce custom proportion, buy ready-made to save money.
#### 4.2.2 Wood Types
1. Ready-made furniture makes实木 accessible.
2. Hardwood vs softwood.
3. Hardwood: North American black walnut — king of North American hardwoods.
#### 4.2.3 Buying Tips
1. Choose实木 when possible; choose hardwood when possible.
2. Online: Yuan's Wood Language, Visa原始木.
3. Price high to low: black walnut, cherry, oak (not rubberwood) [from oak tree], ash, Manchurian ash, beech (山毛榉), rubberwood, pine (softwood), fir.
#### 4.2.4 Why Imported Wood?
1. China's forest coverage is 23% (was 8% in 1949), well below the world average of 31%. China bans natural forest logging and develops fast-growing forests moderately.
#### 4.5.1 Textiles
##### 4.5.1.1 Curtains
1. Choose tracks with curtain boxes; compatible with electric tracks.
2. Install tracks during hard decoration.
3. Polyester (durable, blackout, wrinkle-resistant, easy to clean, airtight, cheap) — for bedrooms needing darkness and quiet.
   Linen (translucent, breathable, long-lasting, hard to clean, shrinks) — for dust-free studies.
   Cotton-linen (similar to linen, softer, translucent, breathable, not sun-resistant, hard to clean, wrinkles, deformable, good value) — for living rooms.
   Silk (luxurious, somewhat fire-resistant, expensive).
   Velvet (good drape, heavy, glossy, blackout, sound-absorbing, insulating, affordable, dust-prone).
   Sheer: thin synthetic fabric; breathable, soft light — for spaces needing no blackout.
4. Curtain height: <2.8m buy standard height, >2.8m buy standard width.
5. Pleat ratio: sheer 1:2.5; fabric 2× for width >4m, 1.8× for <4m.
##### 4.5.1.2 Sofas
1. Don't overspend on sofas — poor value.
2. As long as the fabric is easy to clean, seat depth is generous, and rebound is good — buy online.
##### 4.5.1.3 Mattresses
1. Firm with softness, soft with support.
2. Structure: spring layer, comfort layer, fabric layer.
3. Pocket springs vs open coil; pocket springs isolate motion but are less breathable and wear faster in weight zones; 3-zone is enough — multi-zone is just a sales tactic.
4. Comfort layer: foam, latex, textiles, new materials stacked — thicker = more expensive, but often gimmicky (e.g., 5mm latex = "latex mattress"). Brand: Qizuop.
### 4.6 Appliances
#### 4.6.1 Range Hood
1. Airflow ≥20m³/min sufficient; static pressure 300-400Pa.
2. Side-suction vs top-suction — closer to the pan = better.
3. Look for self-cleaning, one-way exhaust, easy operation.
#### 4.6.2 Gas Stove
1. Choose high heat output; effective heat = load × efficiency; 5kW is high, Grade 1 efficiency ≥66%.
2. Integrated stoves are hard to repair and clean.
#### 4.6.3 Dishwasher
1. Type: built-in.
2. Washing: spray (recommended) vs ultrasonic (high-end).
3. Drying: condensation drying sufficient; crystal drying for ample budget.
4. Capacity: 12 sets sufficient; bigger is better.
#### 4.6.4 Refrigerator
1. Confirm dimensions in advance; choose inverter.
2. Cooling: fan-cooled or fan + direct cooling.
3. Freshness: low temp, high humidity, low oxygen — any保鲜 technology works.
4. Circulation: single (one set for whole fridge), dual (separate fridge/freezer), triple (fridge/freezer/变温). Choose dual or triple.
#### 4.6.5 Washing Machine
1. "Wash ratio" is misleading; choose drum over pulsator.
2. BLDC motor for quiet operation.
3. Look for sterilization function.
#### 4.6.6 Dryer
1. Types: condenser, vented, heat pump — only heat pump gives ready-to-wear results.
#### 4.6.7 Water Heater
1. Capacity, tank material,恒温, zero冷水, exhaust type.
2. 16L normal; oxygen-free copper; water/gas dual modulation恒温; zero冷水 optional; wall-balanced flue.
3. Gas appliances报废 after 8 years.
#### 4.6.8 TV
1. Picture quality: TV > laser TV > projector (laser and projector both project light).
#### Appliance Buying
