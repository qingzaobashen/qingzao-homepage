As someone who has finished renovating and hit many pitfalls, I understand the "the more guides I read, the more confused I get" feeling. Too much information leaves you not knowing what to do. Don't panic.

Renovating isn't to show off — it's about "choosing the most comfortable lifestyle under your current conditions." So spend on "high-frequency use" and "hard-to-replace" things.

I'll break this into three core decision logics. You'll know your mind afterward.

### 1. The golden allocation ratio for hard renovation, soft furnishing, and appliances (reference)

The popular 5:3:2 (hard:soft:appliance) suits ample budgets. **A more pragmatic suggestion is 6:2:2 (more weight on foundational quality).** For a 200k budget: ~100–120k hard, 40–60k soft, 40k appliances. Also keep 10–15% as contingency (early budgets are usually underestimated).

- **Hard renovation (50–60%)**: principle — "safe, durable, eco-friendly, easy to maintain."
    - Prioritize: plumbing/electrical, doors/windows, waterproofing, flooring/tiles, latex paint (mid-range or above, eco-grade).
    - Can save: ceilings (skip complex ones), window sills (use artificial stone), baseboards (match doors, no solid wood needed), kitchen/bath aluminum ceilings (use plain white aluminum, not honeycomb panels).
- **Soft furnishing (20–25%)**: principle — "comfortable, flexible, updatable."
    - Prioritize: mattress, sofa, dining chairs, master bedroom curtains (light-blocking is key), custom cabinets (good multi-ply solid wood carcass, door fronts can be substituted).
    - Can save: all decor (art, ornaments, rugs), accent chairs, cushions. **Don't buy these yet; add slowly after moving in.** Empty beats cluttered.
- **Appliances (15–20%)**: principle — "strong core function, daily high frequency."
    - Prioritize: range hood & cooktop, dishwasher, gas water heater, dryer, smart toilet.
    - Can save: TV (basic model), fridge (choose frost-free inverter large capacity, no need for ice-making or big screen), and any "smart" appliance with excessive premium.

### 2. The most worthwhile spending order: concealed work > high-frequency touchpoints > looks/decor

**1. Most worthwhile, never skimp: the "water, electricity, gas, heating, network" in hard renovation.** This is the skeleton — once enclosed, changes mean breaking walls.
- **Plumbing/electrical points**: kitchen and bathroom outlets — rather more than fewer (smart toilet, disposer, water purifier, robot vacuum hookups). Weak-current (network) cable: Cat 6 or above, pre-route whole-house WiFi conduits.
- **Waterproofing**: bathroom, balcony, kitchen window edges — done poorly harms neighbors, huge rework cost.
- **Hardware**: angle valves, floor drains, hot/cold water joints. Hidden but if they leak, they ruin floors and cabinets. **Buy the best angle valves for tens of yuan — don't skimp.**

**2. Most happiness-boosting: things you touch, use, and sleep on every day.**
- **The "hands" of the home**: door handles, faucets, showerheads, switch plates. Touched dozens of times daily; smooth, warm feel is real quality.
- **The "feet" of the home**: good flooring (laminate or engineered is fine; key is wear layer and locking), good slippers.
- **Body contact surfaces**: mattress (**absolutely most worthwhile!**), sofa cushions, curtain draw feel.
- **Core kitchen/bath appliances**: **range hood & cooktop** (safety & health), **dishwasher** (resolves family conflict), **gas water heater** (constant temp bathing matters), **dryer** (frees the balcony).

**3. Least worth big money: pure decor, easily dated, hard-to-clean things.**
- **Complex ceilings**: trap dust, lower ceiling height; only justify for hiding central-AC lines.
- **Expensive feature walls** (marble, huge upholstered panels): costly, dated fast, boring in two days. Latex paint + art is cheap and fresh.
- **Trendy lighting** (feather, crystal, upward-open shades): dust, dead bugs, hard to wipe.
- **Pricey tiles** (imported slabs): laid on the floor, no one but you will lie down to look. Choose brand specials, pure color, non-slip.
- **Treadmill**: likely becomes a clothes rack.

### 3. Allocate reasonably, don't waste

1. **"Buy finished products rather than custom where possible"**: bathroom vanities, shoe cabinets, desks — many finished sizes fit and are cheap/eco. Custom only for cabinet and wardrobe bodies.
2. **"Movable furniture rather than fixed"**: partition cabinets, banquettes, tatami (especially with lift). These are nearly impossible to re-layout later.
3. **"Buy online, not inflated in physical stores"**: switch plates, lighting, hardware hangers, shower enclosures, toilets, showerheads, curtains. Online prices are transparent with frequent deals.
4. **"Do less of what companies profit most from"**: complex ceilings, feature walls, large demolitions, meaningless shapes.
5. **"Keep 10–15% contingency"**: renovation always has surprises. Best left untouched; if used, no panic.

### Minimalist list (by importance)

1. **Tier 1 (buy with eyes closed)**: plumbing/electrical points & materials, waterproofing, mattress, dishwasher, dryer, gas water heater, smart toilet seat, good faucet & showerhead.
2. **Tier 2 (choose cost-performance)**: tiles, flooring, latex paint, custom cabinets, range hood & cooktop, curtains.
3. **Tier 3 (save where possible)**: ceilings, feature walls, expensive lighting, decor, treadmill, complex curtain boxes.

**"Renovation isn't to show others, but to live comfortably yourself. Spend on what you use every day, not what you glance at once in a while."**

Don't fixate on style first — list your most real **living circulation** (daily: home → change shoes → hang coat → wash hands → cook → eat → sleep). Every pain point on that line is where you should spend.

Wish you a smooth renovation with few pitfalls, and an early move into your ideal home!
