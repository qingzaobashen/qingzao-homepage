## Hiring an Independent Foreman? This Checklist Saves You Pitfalls

#### Phase 1: Where to find one?
- **Get recommendations**
    1. **Ask people you know first**: friends/neighbors/designers whose work you've seen in person — most trustworthy.
    2. **The community owner group is a treasure**: directly ask "any recommended foreman? one who's done several units in our compound?" A foreman familiar with the compound saves worry.
    3. **Be careful online**: on local forums and renovation apps, focus on foremen who post daily site updates over the long term. Accounts with only renderings are suspicious — likely stolen photos.
- **Ask these on the phone first**
    1. **State the basics**: tell the foreman the compound, layout size, and renovation needs (demolition, style); ask if there's a nearby site to view.
    2. **Three must-ask questions**:
        * "Are the workers fixed or临时 hired?" (fixed workers are steadier, more skilled)
        * "How many sites are you managing now?" (best ≤3, or attention spreads thin)
        * "Do you accept third-party supervision?" (refusal = caution)

#### Phase 2: Meet and talk
- **Core questions when meeting**
    1. **Similar experience?**: "Done my type of layout/style? What was hardest, how solved?" (clear answers = real experience)
    2. **How are problems handled?**: "How to rectify bad work? how often on site?" (clear rectification process = responsible)
    3. **Worker situation**: "Who are the key trades — plumbing/electrical, tiler? can I talk to them directly?" (fixed core workers = better quality)
    4. **Liability for accidents**: "Who bears worker safety? if you flood the downstairs neighbor or damage public property, how handled?" (clarify upfront to avoid disputes)
- **Scrutinize the quote (key to avoiding pitfalls)**
    1. **Itemize by space + trade**: e.g., "living room — plumbing/electrical," "kitchen — tiling." Reject a lump-sum "package price" with only a total — easy to hide tricks.
    2. **Check easily-omitted items**:
        * New wall plastering & mesh (without it, walls crack)
        * Floor/wall leveling (uneven floor under flooring = creaks)
        * Door straightening, baseboard install (small jobs most often omitted)
        * Tile chamfering, floor-tile baseboards (detail = aesthetics)
        * Drainpipe soundproof wrapping, access openings (else you hear upstairs flush)
        * AC drilling, slotting (don't wait until AC install to find no hole)
        * Garbage removal to designated spot (else you haul it yourself)
    3. **Plumbing/electrical quote is key**: either per-point or lump-sum; must state "charged by actual measurement, total fluctuation ≤X%." Never sign an open "per-meter actual" contract — it'll compute to your dismay.
    4. **Material brands/models specific**: wire brand, sq mm; waterproof brand, flexible or rigid — all written clearly, not just "wire" or "waterproof."

#### Phase 3: Visit the site
- **Site inspection focus**
    1. **Management**: tidy site? materials stacked neatly? doors/windows/floor protected? (good management = good work)
    2. **Craft details**:
        * **Plumbing/electrical**: conduits straight and secure? strong/weak separated? pipe welds even?
        * **Tiler**: tiles flat? tap for hollow spots? inside/outside corners square? floor drain in a "回"-shape (for fast drainage)?
        * **Carpenter**: gypsum board joints with V-groove? ceiling corners one whole L-board? (crack prevention)
    3. **Chat with workers**: ask "how high is waterproof usually applied?" "what tile joint spacing?" Clear standards = reliable.
    4. **Ask the owner's experience**: if you meet the site owner, privately ask "is the foreman reliable? solves problems fast? easy to talk to?" (owner's words most trustworthy)

#### Phase 4: Sign the contract
- **Sign even if familiar**
    1. **A contract, however simple, is necessary**: use a local renovation contract template; at least state: scope, schedule, total price, payment, responsibility split, acceptance standard, warranty, breach liability.
    2. **Quote and drawings as attachments**: final quote and drawings (if any) attached, both signed — future disputes use them as evidence.
- **Key clauses to agree**
    1. **Don't yield on payment**:
        * Insist on "3331" or more conservative: 30% start, 30% after plumbing/electrical acceptance, 30% after tiling/carpentry acceptance, final 10% after full satisfactory completion.
        * Never sign if ≥60% upfront — too much paid, you're passive, hard to demand fixes.
    2. **Schedule and penalty locked**: explicit start/completion dates; foreman-caused delay penalty per day (e.g., 1‰ of total) protects you.
    3. **Extras stated upfront**: extra work only with your written consent, priced per contract unit; total extras best ≤5% of total, else it's a "fishing project."
    4. **Warranty clear**: concealed work (plumbing/electrical, waterproofing) 5 years, others 2 years; also state specific response like "on-site within 24h of a problem."
- **Finally confirm two things**
    1. **Keep ID**: note the foreman's ID number and phone; best get a regularly-used material supplier contact — indirectly shows if he's long-term.
    2. **Trust your gut**: throughout, is the foreman patient? sincere? organized? One who only says "no problem, leave it to me" without specifics, or is hard to communicate with — pass. You'll spend months together; don't make it miserable.
