# Renovation Contract Signing Notes

I was once a beginner, not understanding the contract's importance. Before renovation we agreed well, but after start, extra charges kept appearing, the schedule slipped, and I argued with the company constantly — even my sleep suffered.

Don't let that happen to you. Sign per this guide; a signed contract is your most important "umbrella" for the coming months.

## Prelude: steady — don't rush to sign
Before picking up the pen, ensure three things are in hand:

1. **Design drawings**: the contract's "constitution." Without settled drawings, the quote and construction are castles in the air. (See the "pre-construction design confirmation" node.)
- The designer may not finish all drawings before signing, but keep their hand-drawn or simplified sketches, including: demolition/new-build, floor plan, plumbing/electrical points, ceiling plan, tile layout, key furniture sizes. Have them complete all drawings soon after and re-confirm.
- **Extra reminder**: verify drawing dimensions exactly match the real space; all design changes must use a signed written change order to avoid later disputes from drawing errors.

2. **Flawless budget list**: the contract's "ledger." It must map 1:1 to drawings, listing **every item's** name, quantity, unit price, craft description, and **brand, model, spec, grade of main and auxiliary materials**. Reject vague "quality xx," "brand xx."
- **Extra reminder**: the budget must include all possible fees — garbage removal, material carrying, management — to avoid later "hidden charges."

3. **An official model contract**: proactively propose the **"Residential Interior Decoration Construction Contract (Model Text)"** jointly issued by your city's **Housing and Urban-Rural Development Commission** and **Market Supervision Administration**. It's relatively fair and covers most basic clauses.
- **Extra reminder**: the official model may lack detail in places; add supplementary clauses per your needs so all key agreements hit paper.

## Core clauses, word by word — where your "magnifier" should shine

### Clause 1: Project overview — the "ID"
- **State clearly**: property address, built area, contract type (half/full), period (specific start and completion dates).
- **One-line rule**: **"who, where, how long, how"** — unambiguous.

### Clause 2: Payment — your "leverage"
Most important clause, directly tied to control of all later stages.
- **Traps**: too-high down payment (>60%); payment milestones detached from progress (e.g., "40% before carpentry" regardless of quality).
- **Ideal ratio**: the industry-recognized **"3331"** is worth referencing:
    - **30%** start deposit
    - **30%** after plumbing/electrical done and accepted
    - **30%** after tiling/carpentry (tile, ceiling) done and accepted
    - **final 10%** after all works accepted and site cleared
- **Core principle**: money always one step behind progress — use payment to drive and verify quality.
- **Extra reminder**: define each payment's precondition, e.g., "plumbing/electrical accepted" needs a signed acceptance sheet, to avoid "verbally accepted" demands for payment.

### Clause 3: Schedule & delay — lock time in
- **Clear count**: from __ year __ month __ day to __ year __ month __ day, total __ calendar days.
- **Penalty**: state that contractor-caused delay incurs **1‰ of total contract price per day (or agreed amount)** — an effective constraint.
- **Extra reminder**: define delay liability — owner-caused delay (late materials, design change) exempts contractor, avoiding later wrangling.

### Clause 4: Quality & acceptance — measurable standards
- **No empty words**: not just "meets national standards." Attach **specific acceptance standards**, e.g.:
    - "Wall flatness error ≤3mm" (2m straightedge)
    - "Tile hollow rate <5%, center must not be hollow"
    - "Drain pipes flow smoothly, no leak"
- **Acceptance flow**: state "after each stage (plumbing/electrical, tiling/carpentry, paint, completion), owner or owner-hired third-party supervisor must accept and sign before next stage and payment."
- **Extra reminder**: define handling of failed acceptance — "failed items rectified within 3 working days and re-applied; rectification doesn't extend schedule."

### Clause 5: Material supply — no "substitution"
- **Main & auxiliary list**: the budget attachment above, detailed. Even a screw, if branded, must be noted.
- **Right to inspect**: state "all incoming materials, owner may inspect; if non-conforming, contractor replaces unconditionally, no schedule extension, bears liability."
- **Extra reminder**: define material inspection flow — "contractor notifies owner 24h ahead; usable only after acceptance" — avoid uninspected materials.

### Clause 6: Changes — lock the "extra-charge" cage
Extras are the biggest budget black hole — strictly controlled.
- **Process lock**: state "**all changes (add or reduce) require owner's signed written Change Order before work. No verbal promises or unauthorized changes.**"
- **Cost lock**: add "**this budget is a fixed-price contract; final settlement increase ≤5% (or agreed ratio) of budget total.**" This curbs low-entry-then-surge chaos.
- **Extra reminder**: Change Order must state change content, cost adjustment, schedule impact, so both agree and avoid disputes.

### Clause 7: Warranty — future "peace-of-mind insurance"
- **Itemized warranty**: different items, different periods, stated separately. National basic is 2 years, but fight for longer on key items:
    - Waterproofing/leak-proof: **≥5 years**
    - Plumbing/electrical concealed: **5 years**
    - Other: **2 years**
- **Response time**: state "on quality issue, contractor responds within **24h, on-site within 48h**" to avoid delay.
- **Extra reminder**: define warranty scope and exclusions, e.g., "damage from owner misuse or third party not covered" — avoid unclear liability.

## Signing actions: final step, steady
1. **Enough time**: don't be rushed to "sign in 5 minutes." Give at least an hour to read word by word, especially blanks to fill.
2. **Fill all blanks**: contract's blank lines, especially amount/date/percentage — fill specifically or cross with "/" to prevent later tampering.
3. **Verbal promises on paper**: all extra promises ("3 free cleanings," "free upgrade to brand X pipe") handwritten in blanks as "supplementary clauses," signed/stamped by both.
4. **Sealed attachments**: every attachment (drawings, budget, material list) must have the contractor's **overlap or official seal on each page** to be legally equal to the main contract.
5. **Keep the original**: you must keep a **complete, sealed, signed original contract and all attachments** — your root for rights protection.

## Final: trust and rules coexist
Signing a rigorous contract isn't malicious suspicion, but building clearer trust atop rules. When both parties' rights and duties are brought into the light, cooperation goes steadier and farther.
