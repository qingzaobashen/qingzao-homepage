### Hiring an Independent Designer? Lock It In With 4 Steps

#### Phase 1: Check background, view works
- **Find via reliable channels**
    - Best: referrals from acquaintances; then certified design platforms; or directly ask who designed a friend's nice home.
    - Search their name + "design" on social media to see old-client reviews and real interactions.
- **Review the portfolio**
    - Focus on complete real finished cases from the last 2 years, confirming they're still actively designing.
    - See if their style is fixed or varied, judging fit with your needs.
    - Don't only look at large layouts; small/awkward layouts better show problem-solving ability.
- **Ask professional background**
    - Know their training (architecture, environmental art — formal education better) and past companies.
    - Ask "why go solo?" to infer their pursuit of the craft and stability.

#### Phase 2: Talk deeply, assess ability
- **Must-ask questions**
    1. "In your recent projects, what's the design you're proudest of? what problem did it solve for the owner?" (depth of design)
    2. "How do you help control budget? when limited, which part do you prioritize?" (cost-saving ability and values)
    3. "When my idea conflicts with your professional advice, how do you handle it?" (communication and attitude)
- **Clarify service scope**
    - Confirm scope: on-site measurement, full construction drawings, renderings, material selection accompaniment, soft-furnishing list, site handover/acceptance.
    - How many site visits during construction? which key nodes (plumbing/electrical, carpentry, completion) must he attend?
    - How many projects now? enough time/energy for yours?
- **Spell out fees and payment**
    - Design fee by built area or usable area? does total include all services?
    - Payment method? usually deposit + concept phase + construction-drawing phase + final; keep final ≥20%.
    - Construction follow-up fee? fixed amount or % of project?

#### Phase 3: Assess execution ability
- **Ask about construction cooperation**
    - Directly: "Do you have a long-term reliable foreman? can you take me to an active site? can you give professional opinions on the construction?"
    - If a fixed partner foreman, must personally visit the active site to gauge quote level.
    - If you find the crew yourself, ask if the designer can give screening criteria and help interview foremen.
- **Check drawing details**
    - Request the full construction set of a finished project, focusing on:
        - Detailed elevation drawings, detail drawings, plumbing/electrical point drawings?
        - Are materials clearly marked — brand, series, model, spec?
    - Drawing detail directly decides whether the crew can quote and work accurately — key to avoiding extras and deviation.
- **Understand resources**
    - Any partner building-material merchants, whole-house cabinetry, furniture buyers?
    - Are these pure referrals or can they get discounts/priority service? (beware designers earning high kickbacks on materials)

#### Phase 4: Sign contract, prevent risk
- **Sign a formal contract**
    - Use a standard template; state service scope, drawing count, delivery time, revision count, mutual responsibility.
    - Add: "Designer is responsible for handing over to the crew and giving professional opinions on whether construction meets the design."
- **Agree on design changes and extras**
    - After the plan is set, how charged if the owner makes major changes?
    - During construction, if on-site issues require design adjustment, how fast to respond? how handled?
- **Get along well**
    - Respect the designer's professional opinion while clearly stating your needs.
    - Set fixed communication times (e.g., weekly progress sync); don't interrupt randomly.
    - Trust matters, but all important agreements must be on paper — words alone prove nothing.

#### High-risk signals (caution if encountered)
- Refuses to show full construction drawings of past projects.
- Keeps recommending unknown material/custom brands you can't buy yourself.
- Says "find any crew yourself, I'll cooperate" but gives zero crew-screening advice.
- Handling 5+ projects at once with no assistant.
- Only talks style/concepts, dodges construction details.

Hiring an independent designer means choosing a long-term "professional advisor." Responsibility, time/energy, and execution experience matter more than pure creative flair. Spend more time evaluating upfront and state the cooperation rules clearly, and you'll hit fewer pitfalls later.
