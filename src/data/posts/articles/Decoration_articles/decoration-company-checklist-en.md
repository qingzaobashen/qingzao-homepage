# How to Choose a Renovation Company: Prevent Extra Charges, Avoid Schedule Traps, Ensure Quality

Choosing a renovation company must be done with extreme care. The wrong one not only blows the budget but may also bring quality and schedule-delay problems, raising the difficulty of the whole game. This is a purely practical article with a question checklist — just ask along the lines.

---

## Phase 1: Online screening — know your own needs

Before contacting any company, know your real needs, budget, and timeline:

### 1. Clarify your core needs
- **Set a budget range**: total = hard renovation (labor + auxiliary + main materials) + soft furnishing (furniture, cabinets) + appliances, roughly 4:3:3. Keep **10–15%** as contingency.
  - **Tip**: research at building-material markets or ask recently-renovated friends for more accurate ratios.
- **Choose a model**: know the three mainstream models
  - **Half-package**: company supplies auxiliary materials + labor, high autonomy.
  - **Full-package**: company supplies auxiliary + labor + main materials, worry-free.
  - **All-in-one**: adds design, construction, main materials, soft furnishing, even appliances — one-stop.
  - **Tip**: ample budget + tight time → full-package/all-in-one; some knowledge + time → half-package.
- **Sort a detailed needs list**:
  - Household members and special needs (elderly, kids, pets).
  - Must-fix layout pain points.
  - Preferred design style.
  - Core material brand leanings (flooring, tiles).
  - Habits (cooking frequency, entertainment/office needs).
  - Priority: function or aesthetics?
  - **Tip**: collect favorite case photos to convey preferences intuitively.

### 2. Multi-channel online screening
- **Use platforms**: on TubaTuba, Zhuxiaobang, etc., enter city/layout/budget to match local companies.
  - **Tip**: pick higher-rated companies with many cases matching your style.
- **Budget filter**: by your budget and model, shortlist roughly fitting companies (mid-high-end, good hard-quality reputation).
- **Search real reputation**: on Xiaohongshu, Dianping, search "company name + site + fail + real review", focusing on construction detail and problem-handling reviews.
  - **Tip**: spot fake reviews; real owners include specific construction details and personal experience.
- **Verify basics**: via Tianyancha or local housing/industry associations, check business license and litigation/penalty records.
  - **Tip**: focus on renovation-quality and contract-dispute lawsuits.
- **Initial contact**: after adding WeChat, ask key questions to filter:
  - "Do you do half/full/all-in-one?"
  - "Free on-site measurement?"
  - "Detailed itemized quote?"
  - "Accept third-party independent supervision?" (refusal or extra charge hints weak standards)
  - "How is design fee charged? Does the designer follow up on-site during construction?" (beware "free design" — designer often vanishes after start)

* Goal of this phase: shortlist 3–5 apparently reliable, direction-matching companies.*

---

## Phase 2: Visit the company — understand deeply

An on-site visit feels the company's strength and the designer's professionalism. They'll tour you, explain materials/craft, then the designer details your layout. Bring your needs and the question list below:

### 1. Examine the company basics
1. **Qualifications & honors**: check business license, decoration engineering qualification, industry association membership.
  - **Tip**: confirm validity and that scope includes residential renovation.
2. **Team & cases**: average experience of designers, especially similar-layout/style experience. Ask for **complete real finished cases in your community**, not just renderings.
  - **Tip**: ask the case's specific difficulties and solutions to gauge ability.
3. **Materials & craft display**:
  - Observe the material showroom — are auxiliary material brands (wire, pipe, putty) clear?
  - Glance at main material displays for styles you like.
  - Check the company's **standardized construction craft** (wall crack prevention, plumbing/electrical routing standards).
  - **Tip**: photograph brands and standards for later comparison.

### 2. Deep talk with the designer
- **Professionalism**: a good designer proactively asks about habits (cooking frequency, storage, pets/kids/elderly) and gives specific advice.
  - Ask: "For a home with elderly/kids, what safety or convenience details would you consider?"
  - Imagine if the circulation fits the family.
**Detailed question list**:
- **Fees**: beware vague or far-below-market "package prices"; ask item by item:
  1. Quote by built area or usable area?
  2. Design fee amount? discounts?
  3. Plumbing/electrical: point-to-point or per-meter? upfront deposit? (best push for a "lump sum")
  4. Management, garbage removal, stair-carry fees?
  5. Payment ratio and milestones? (ideal: 30% contract, 30% plumbing/electrical acceptance, 30% tiling/carpentry acceptance, 10% completion)
  6. Schedule length? delay penalty?
  7. How many meters in the cabinet package? overage how calculated?
  8. Does package include balcony waterproofing? how charged?
  9. Cornice charged?
  10. Can you sign a full-package fixed-price contract with extra charges capped at 5%?
  11. Large tile laying how charged?
  12. How many paint colors? overage?
  13. Which items are personalized? how charged?
- **Push on construction & material details**:
  1. How to ensure result matches drawings? (does the company have a **full execution** mechanism)
  2. What does half-package include? or full-package package contents?
  3. Are workers company-owned or outsourced? who bears worker safety/damage?
  4. What's the supervision mechanism? can an unsatisfactory foreman be replaced?
  5. How many waterproof coats? how high? (bathroom wall at least 1.8m)
  6. Bathroom backfill material?
  7. How many putty/paint coats? wait for dry between?
  8. Wire mesh at old/new wall joints?
  9. Concealed-work warranty length?
  10. All materials to exact brand, series, model.
  11. Materials eco-friendly? test reports available?
  12. Showroom main materials free choice or tiered? (beware low-end goods of big brands)

### 3. Check the quote for omissions
Request a detailed budget list and verify it includes:
1. Wall scraping, wall demolition.
2. Floor/wall leveling (especially where flooring or custom cabinets go).
3. Waterproofing (area and height).
4. Plumbing/electrical slotting, strong/weak box relocation, switch/socket count.
5. Tile laying (non-standard sizes, chamfering, thin-set extra?).
6. Ceilings (are design shapes extra?).
7. Epoxy grout, pipe wrapping, curtain boxes.
8. Threshold stones, window sills, baseboards.
9. Wall mesh, interface agent (wall primer).

**Key reminder**: at this point companies often pressure you to sign. If not fully satisfied, no matter the discount, don't rush — as long as you don't sign, discounts keep coming daily.

## Phase 3: Site inspection — test real capability

Pick 1–2 candidates for site inspection. The "model home" is the seller's show; the active site is the real buyer's show.

### 1. Choose the right phase
Prioritize **finished plumbing/electrical** (exposed conduits, not yet enclosed) and **tiling/carpentry in progress** — these two phases expose the most craft issues.

### 2. Inspect the site
- **Site management & protection**: tidy? materials sorted? entry door, windows, installed equipment protected?
- **Craft details**:
  - **Plumbing/electrical**: conduits straight? foil shielding at strong/weak crossings? pipe welds neat?
  - **Tiler**: tiles flat, hollow rate? inside/outside corners square?
  - **Carpenter**: ceiling corners using one whole L-shaped board to prevent cracks?
- **Talk to on-site staff**: ask workers "Does the company train you uniformly?" or "What's the standard for this step?" Judge professionalism and management from answers.
  - Note their demeanor — reflects management.
- **Verify material authenticity**: check on-site materials (wire, pipe, board) match promised brand/model. Ask for delivery slips or certificates.
  - Beware fakes or low-end OEM goods.
- **Talk to the owner**: if possible, ask about crew cooperation and response speed; even better if they reveal the cost.
  - Tip: leave the owner's contact for later detail.

**Focus**: **site tidiness and standardization** directly reflect management and attitude. **Concealed-work details** (plumbing/electrical routing, waterproofing) are core to future safety — never careless.

## Phase 4: Contract negotiation — lock details and rights

The final step to put all promises in writing and legal form. (See "Contract Signing Notes" for details.)

### 1. Prep before signing
- **Finalize design**: before the construction contract, finalize all drawings (plan, elevation, renderings, construction). Quote is based on final drawings; big decisions (ceiling? floor heating?) locked early or extra charges follow.
- **Get the final quote**: detailed quote listing all items, quantities, unit prices, craft, and material brands/models based on finalized drawings.
- **Bargain reasonably**:
  - Cut low-skill items: carrying, demolition, management.
  - Cut non-standard step prices: door straightening, drilling.
  - Don't randomly cut high-skill items (plumbing/electrical, tiles, paint).
- **Fight for extras**: if total won't drop, seek material upgrades (pipe from PPR to antibacterial) or added services (more renderings) or free appliances.

### 2. Use a standard contract
- **Official model contract**: propose the local market regulator & consumer council's "Residential Interior Decoration Construction Contract (Model Text)". Fair on rights, acceptance, warranty.
- **Check attachments item by item**: the core is attachments — ensure **design drawings, budget quote, material list** (brand, model, spec, qty, eco grade) are attached and fully consistent, no vague descriptions.

### 3. Focus on clauses
- **Payment**: reject high upfront (e.g., >60%). Push "**3331**" or similar (30% start, 30% plumbing/electrical acceptance, 30% tiling/carpentry acceptance, 10% completion), tying payment to acceptance.
  - Avoid one-time large payment to reduce risk.
- **Extra charges & changes**: explicitly "**changes require both parties' written confirmation, else invalid**". Best sign a fixed-price contract capping extras at **5%** of total.
  - Tip: state "owner may refuse payment for unjustified extras" to prevent malicious add-ons.
- **Schedule & delay**: explicit start/completion dates and a **daily penalty** for contractor delay (usually ~1‰ of total), ensuring reciprocal liability.
  - Note: check for force-majeure (pandemic, extreme weather) handling.
- **Warranty**: explicit periods (waterproofing ≥5yr, concealed 2–5yr, surface 2yr) and **after-sales response** (e.g., respond within 24h, on-site within 48h).
  - Request a written warranty card.
- **Supplementary clauses**: put the designer's verbal promises in writing, signed/stamped, to avoid later disputes.
  - Tip: record verbal promises on phone as supplementary evidence.
- **Dispute resolution**: clarify "if materials differ from agreement, how handled?" "if acceptance disputed, what's the process?"
  - Advise arbitration or litigation over biased internal mediation.

---

## Summary: the company-selection flow

Clarify needs → online screening (3–5) → visit with "question list" → compare plans/quotes → site inspection (1–2) → finalize design then sign → scrutinize clauses & attachments.

Renovation is a practice that elevates wisdom and ability. May you find a satisfying renovation company.
