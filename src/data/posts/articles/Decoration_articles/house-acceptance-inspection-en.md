## Home Acceptance & Inspection: Inspect Before You Accept — A Beginner's Pitfall Checklist

### Step 1: What to demand at acceptance

- **Inspect before you pay**: Insist on inspecting the home before signing and paying. Try to go on a rainy day — that's when leaks and seepage become obvious. The fees you'll pay usually include the maintenance fund, property management fee, and garbage removal fee.
- **Ask for the "three certificates, one license, one form"**: When taking delivery, the developer must present: the Residential Quality Guarantee, the Residential Usage Manual, the Construction Quality Certificate, the Comprehensive Completion Acceptance Certificate for the development project, and the Completion Acceptance Filing Form.
- **The inspection sheet**: The developer will usually give you a defect list to fill in. Finding problems and noting them is the point — inspection exists to fix issues before you move in. It's the developer's obligation and your responsibility to your future life.
- **Don't panic**: Most problems are common and fixable. Our goal is to be informed and avoid disputes later.
- **Bring a helper**: If possible, bring a friend with renovation experience, or hire a professional inspector for a few hundred yuan. They have proper tools and experience, which is much safer.

---

### Step 2: Bring the right gear

You don't need professional instruments — a few small tools are enough:

1. **Hollow-spot hammer**: Check walls and floors for hollow spots.
2. **Lighter or a small piece of paper**: Test door and window seals.
3. **Your phone**: The most important tool! Use it to photograph and record problems, and it can also act as:
    - A flashlight: light up corners to see if walls are uneven.
    - A level: many phone apps have this — check if windowsills and countertops are level.
    - A receptacle tester: plug it into sockets to confirm power.
4. **Colored sticky notes or chalk**: Mark problems on the spot for easy re-checking.
5. **Tape measure**: Verify room dimensions.
6. **Paper and pen**: Record issues.

---

### Step 3: Inspect zone by zone, like a detective

#### Zone 1: Entry door and windows — the face and eyes of the home
- **Look and touch**: Check the door leaf and window frames for scratches, dents, and even paint. Open and close them a few times — are they smooth, any strange noises?
- **Check the seal**: Close doors and windows, hold a lighter or paper strip near the gap, and see if the flame or paper moves — that tells you how tight the seal is.
- **Test the locks**: Use the key to check if the lock is smooth. Can the window latch lock securely?

#### Zone 2: Walls and ceiling — the skin of the home
- **Tap**: Lightly run the hollow-spot hammer over walls and ceiling. A hollow "thud" means a hollow spot — mark it, as it may crack or fall off later.
- **Shine light**: Turn on your phone flashlight and hold it against the wall. Side light clearly reveals bumps, cracks, and patch marks.
- **Check for seepage**: Focus on the ceiling, bathroom wall corners, and around windows. Yellowing, bubbling, or moldy water stains may mean leakage from upstairs or the exterior wall.

#### Zone 3: Floor and drainage — the foundation and veins of the home
- **Walk around**: Walk several laps on the floor. Feel for unevenness or looseness. Are the tile joints flat and even?
- **Test drainage**: Quickly pour a basin of water into the floor drains in the kitchen, bathroom, and balcony. See if it flows smoothly, with no pooling or backflow. The sound of "gurgling" drainage is ideal.
- **Check pipes**: Inspect all drainpipes and faucets. Are the joints leaking? Feel if the pipe connections are dry.

#### Zone 4: Electrical and fixtures — the nerves and organs of the home
- **Test sockets**: Plug your phone charger or a receptacle tester into every socket to confirm power. A small night light is handy for testing.
- **Check the distribution box**: Open it. Are the switch labels clear? Flip each one to see if it controls the corresponding zone.
- **Test fixtures**: For finished/luxury homes, try the pre-installed range hood, cooktop, air conditioner, and fresh-air system to see if they start normally.

---

### Final step: Record and communicate

After inspection, the most important things are:

1. **Organize your records**: Turn the sticky-note problems, plus photos and videos, into a clear issue list.
2. **Communicate in person**: Hand the list to the developer or property staff, confirm face to face, and ask clearly when things will be fixed.
3. **Get written confirmation**: Best to have them sign and stamp the list, or submit it through the property app or email so there's a record — this is your basis for follow-up.

Remember: don't sign the "Home Acceptance Handover Form" lightly until you're satisfied the problems are resolved.

### A gentle reminder

Inspecting your new home is like a health check-up — it's to let it accompany you in better condition. Even if you find problems, take them in stride; it's just a normal part of taking delivery.
