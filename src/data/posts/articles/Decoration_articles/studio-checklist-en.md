# How to Choose a Design Studio — A 4-Step Evaluation

Unlike traditional renovation companies, a design studio's strength is unique creative ideas and executable personalized plans — most importantly, aesthetics that match yours. Small firms like "XXX Design Studio" or "XXX Space" are what we call design studios.

## Phase 1: Online pre-screening
- **Find info via multiple channels**
  - On Dianping, Yidoutang, Xiaohongshu, search local design studios and read real old-client reviews, especially after-sales attitude.
  - Don't just look at pretty renderings; find posts tagged "full execution," "real finished case," "graduation photos" — those actually get built.
  - Ask recently-renovated friends or local renovation groups for recommendations; real word-of-mouth beats ads.
  - Use Tianyancha to check for business anomalies and avoid risk early.
- **Review the portfolio**
  - Browse the studio's site/portfolio, shortlist 3–5 whose style fits your preference.
  - Focus: does the design consider life details? enough storage? reasonable lighting? coordinated materials?
  - For liked cases, note questions to ask the designer in person.
- **Clarify needs and budget**
  - List: household size, habits, layout changes, must-have appliances, preferred style (keywords suffice).
  - Total budget split into design fee, hard renovation, main materials, soft furnishing. A studio may run ~30% higher than a company — the price of personalization.
- **Initial contact round**
  - Ask via WeChat/phone:
    1. Do you take projects in my community, this area?
    2. How is the design fee charged? what's included? (how many drawings, how many site visits)
    3. Who does construction? their own workers, long-term partners, or do I find my own?
  - Reject anyone pushing to sign or pay a large deposit without sufficient communication!

## Phase 2: Meet and talk
- **Visit the studio in person**
  - Go yourself to feel the working vibe and taste.
  - Talk to the lead designer, not sales! Confirm he'll lead your project end-to-end to avoid mid-way swaps.
- **Needs interview**
  - Lay out your needs list; see if he listens patiently and probes life details.
  - A reliable designer proactively asks: what time do you wake, need a breakfast prep counter? lots of stuff, need floor-to-ceiling storage? love hosting at home? pets needing space? need a home office? — not just pushing trendy templates.
- **Hear initial ideas**
  - Have the designer face your floor plan and explain changes and space planning.
  - Focus on how he solves real problems: brighten a dark room? optimize circulation? use corner space?
  - Ask: "Which part of this plan costs most? any cheaper alternative?" See if he considers your budget, not just grandeur.
- **See complete cases**
  - Ask for 1–2 complete cases from drawing to reality, ideally similar to your layout/needs.
  - Ask details: how long built? how much spent? vs budget? biggest problem and how solved? what the owner finds most useful after moving in? any dissatisfaction?

## Phase 3: Site inspection
- **Ask about construction management**
  - Directly: "If we design with you, how is construction guaranteed to follow the drawings?"
  - If using a partner crew, see their info and past cases; best contact previous owners about quality.
- **See active sites**
  - Skip model homes; ask for 1–2 sites doing plumbing/electrical or tiling/carpentry — that shows real level.
  - Focus:
    1. Site management: materials tidy? protected? garbage cleared promptly?
    2. Craft: conduits straight? wires in conduit? concealed work rough?
    3. Drawings followed: does the site match the design? any cutting corners?
  - Chat with the foreman or workers — do they understand the designer's intent and convey it accurately?
- **Confirm designer site presence**
  - Ask how many times the designer visits during construction? key nodes (handover, acceptance) mandatory.
  - Who to contact if issues arise? how resolved? what's the designer's responsibility?
  - Will he provide detailed material lists and soft-furnishing advice? these help execution.

## Phase 4: Sign the contract
- **Hash out fees**
  - Design fee: by what area? how many installments? limited revision count? includes later soft-furnishing consulting?
  - Construction fee: if via studio's crew, need detailed budget; how is plumbing/electrical priced? best sign a fixed-price contract to avoid malicious extras.
  - Other fees: engineering management, design-tracking fee? ratio? what's included?
- **Review the design contract**
  - Attach a detailed service list specifying:
    1. How many drawings? (at least full construction set, key detail drawings)
    2. What services? (how many renderings, material-selection trips, site handovers)
    3. When each phase delivers; late-delivery penalty?
- **Sign the construction contract**
  - Bind the construction contract to the final construction drawings and budget as attachments.
  - Add: "Crew must strictly build to drawings; designer has final interpretive authority over on-site craft and effect."
  - Make design execution a core acceptance standard to avoid corner-cutting.
- **Final call**
  - When comparing studios: does the design fit, can it execute, is communication smooth, is price reasonable?
  - Trust your instinct! Pick the designer who best understands your needs and feels reliable — you'll work together a long time, so smooth communication matters.
  - Everything verbally promised MUST go in the contract — empty words prove nothing!

## Pitfall tips
- Check the studio's business license and qualification; confirm scope includes interior design for legal operation.
- Focus on cases similar to your needs — same layout, style, budget — don't be distracted by unrelated pretty designs.
- Know the team setup; confirm your designer follows through with a stable partner crew, avoiding chaotic last-minute outsourcing.
- Write service scope and fee standards clearly; all items in black and white as contract attachments to avoid later charges.
- The contract must be detailed! design plan, construction time, material standards, acceptance standards, warranty period all specified.
- During renovation, proactively communicate with designer and foreman weekly to adjust needs and solve problems in time.
- Don't go cheap! Quotes far below market almost certainly hide pitfalls; too-low pricing likely means extra charges during construction to make up the difference.
