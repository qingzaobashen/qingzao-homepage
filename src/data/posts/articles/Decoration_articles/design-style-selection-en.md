# How to Choose a Renovation Style

## 1. Talk about lifestyle first, then decoration style
Before choosing a style, the first question should be: what is your lifestyle?

**From household structure**:
- Singles or young couples seeking simplicity and easy care — modern minimalist and Nordic are often good choices.
- Multi-generation households need to balance different age groups' habits; light French and new Chinese find balance between comfort and ritual.
- Families with kids or pets must mind material choice — avoid all-white cream styles that show dirt easily; use wear-resistant materials and more lively palettes.

**From living habits**:
- Cook at home often? be cautious with open-kitchen-led styles; prioritize easy-clean tiles and strong-range hoods.
- Obsessed with storage? modern minimalist and Japanese MUJI, with hidden cabinets, fit perfectly.
- Entertain friends at home? need open social space. Need a home office? leave room for a study/work zone.

Once you sort out your lifestyle from these angles, style choice becomes a dialogue with yourself, not blind following.

## 2. Quick overview of mainstream styles
After clarifying lifestyle, learn the traits and fit of mainstream styles.

### 1. Modern Minimalist
"Less is more" — functionality and simplicity. Colors: white, gray, wood; smooth lines, no complex shapes; micro-cement, slab, metal, glass. The大众 favorite, fits any layout. Small spaces feel open; large spaces gain texture via soft furnishing. Base cost ~800–1200 RMB/㎡. Suited to office workers, beginners, minimalist families — the most mainstream (modern minimalist leads at 66.89%).
**Pitfall**: minimalism demands structure, wall flatness, and clean joints; poor craft becomes "shabby." Needs strong storage habit — use the "hide 80%, show 20%" rule.

### 2. Nordic Natural
Fresh, natural, bright; light wood, white walls, soft colors; values natural light with cotton/linen soft furnishings and plants. Fits small/mid layouts, warm lively vibe, great for young families with kids. Needs good light. Affordable ~1000–1500 RMB/㎡.

### 3. New Chinese
Blends tradition and modern — symmetric layout, grilles, landscape mood, but simplifies carving, adds glass/metal. Colors: deep brown, ink black, off-white with solid-wood furniture and plants. Suits 120㎡+ or tradition lovers; became top choice for villas, 2025 search up 40%. New Chinese 2.0 modernizes — Ming-style tea table with wireless charging, holographic landscape art. Quality budget ~1500–2500 RMB/㎡.
**Pitfall**: avoid overly heavy/pressing shapes; use minimal grille wardrobes and solid-wood tea tables; traditional elements ≤30%.

### 4. Light Luxury
"Refined not flashy" — light base with metal (brass, stainless), slab, glass. Clean furniture, simple high-end soft furnishing. Colors: black/white/gray, cream gold, champagne. Fits 100㎡+ mid-large layouts, quality-seeking youth. Higher budget ~1500–2500 RMB/㎡.
**Pitfall**: metal ≤10%; furniture by "three looks" — lines (no complex carving), material (top-grain leather/suede), detail (hidden zipper/seamless stitch).

### 5. Wabi-Sabi
Emphasizes emptiness and "beauty of imperfection" — rough materials, gray tones, minimal lines, natural texture; quiet healing. Core colors: gray, earth, matte black. For owners who love pure, calm, artistic atmospheres. But demands very high material/craft, costly (~1800–2500 RMB/㎡), overall gray/dim — poor light turns it into a "rough shell."
**Pitfall**: don't blindly pursue whole-house micro-cement; use matte tiles instead (smooth, easy-clean); walls with ordinary latex paint to avoid dirty/pressing rough materials.

### 6. Cream Style
Soft cream tones (beige, light coffee, milky white) with clean lines and delicate soft furnishing — warm romantic. Materials: sheepskin velvet, cotton/linen, rattan, wood. Popular on Xiaohongshu for healing; ~1000–1800 RMB/㎡. But its tones and curved shapes are time-sensitive.
**Pitfall**: use "neutral hard + styled soft" — neutral walls/floor, express cream only via replaceable elements (arched mirror, round side table); avoid full "cream-ization." Below 2.6m ceiling, skip complex cornices.

### 7. Industrial
From early-20th-century factories/warehouses — raw material texture, exposed brick, metal pipes, concrete. 2025's industrial evolved — less exposed steel/red brick, more micro-cement, wood veneer, textiles, low-saturation tones, balancing Loft openness with comfort. Fits large layouts or youth seeking individuality; wood furniture balances the cold.

### 8. Other popular styles
Beyond the seven: **Japanese wood** (fresh, ordered, white+beige+light wood) is a rising favorite. **French light-luxury** features refined cornices, carving, arched openings with marble/velvet/leather. **Mid-century** blends retro and modern with warm retro tones, wood furniture, retro lighting — for youth seeking individual experience.

## 3. How to make the final choice
Facing many styles, lock your direction in four steps.

### Step 1: Collect inspiration, build an image library
Browse favorite cases on home platforms (Xiaohongshu, Haohaozhu, Pinterest); save what you love.

Key: save by space — living room, bedroom, bathroom, kitchen separately — to convey ideas accurately to the designer later.

### Step 2: Extract patterns from images
Look at collected images: **find repeated elements and colors** — recurring wood furniture, white walls, arches, metal lines? These repetitions are your aesthetic code.

### Step 3: Filter against reality
Match preferences to actual conditions:
- **Layout size**: small (<80㎡) → Nordic, Japanese, cream; large (>120㎡) → new Chinese, light luxury.
- **Light**: poor light → light styles (Nordic, cream); good light → dark styles (industrial, wabi-sabi).
- **Budget**: economy (≤800/㎡) → modern minimalist, Nordic; quality (≥1500/㎡) → light luxury, new Chinese.

### Step 4: Durability test
Ask four questions:
1. Will I still like it in three years?
2. How costly to change style elements?
3. Does this style truly match my habits?
4. Will it affect future resale value?
These separate "fleeting liking" from "lasting love."

## 4. Style mixing techniques
More people now mix rather than stick to one style. 2025 data: mixed styles are mainstream at 65%. The core is proportion and coordination, not piling favorites. Five principles:

**Principle 1: Pick a main style, accent with others**
Set a "base style" (~70%), accent with others (~30%). E.g., Nordic base + industrial iron lighting and leather chair; or MUJI base + light American moldings and metal handles.

**Principle 2: Unified color system, no jarring jumps**
Styles may mix, but tones shouldn't split. Pick ≤3 main colors + 1 accent; "same hue, different lightness" or "neutral + one saturated pop." Safe combos: wood+white+gray, milky+deep green, off-white+deep blue.

**Principle 3: Material echo, not patchwork**
Ensure material texture, temperature, mood echo. E.g., Nordic+industrial via same wood veneer and black iron; Japanese+modern via fair-faced concrete walls and wood furniture to reduce cold.

**Principle 4: Soft furnishing led, hard minimal**
The soul of mixing is often in soft furnishing — furniture, lighting, curtains, cushions, rugs. No need to move walls; furniture alone creates the mix.

**Principle 5: Zone by function, mind transitions**
Different zones can express different styles — e.g., warm Nordic living room, slightly retro-American dining, wabi-sabi quiet study. Key is smooth "transition" via material gradient, tone continuity, or lighting.

## Final words
Recent styles show clear diversification and personalization. The trend itself is shifting — from "minimalism" to a more "human, warm" post-minimalism, with eco-awareness and smart home fully reshaping design philosophy.
